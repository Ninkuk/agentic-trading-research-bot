# BigBear.ai Holdings (NYSE: BBAI) — 2026-07-21

## 1. Verdict and thesis

**Pass. I would not own this at $2.94.**

BigBear.ai is a shrinking federal services contractor with an AI label on it: the
organic business declined **−18.8% year over year** in the most recent quarter, and the
only growing asset it owns was bought for **$272.1M of cash** and still accounts for just
**17.9% of revenue**. The market pays **11.1× declining trailing sales** for a business
that has never generated positive free cash flow, that has issued shares at a rate which
cut revenue per share by **78% in four years**, and that on 2026-06-09 doubled its
authorized share count from 500M to 1.0B — going from 4.4% issuance headroom to 108.8%.

**Kill-thesis verdict: UNPROVEN** — nothing was refuted, but one of the four
load-bearing conditions **cannot be attacked at all** because the disclosure needed does
not exist (see §8). **Load-bearing conditions: 4** (§5).

That verdict does not overturn the pass; it sharpens what the pass is. I am not saying
"this company will fail." I am saying **I cannot tell**, on public disclosure, whether
Ask Sage's growth is durable enough to outrun the decline — and I decline to own a
$1.4B security whose entire case rests on a number the company does not publish.

This is a pass, not a short call. I have no view on the stock's direction — 29.4% of the
float is short, implied vol is 92.9%, and a name like that can do anything over a
three-month horizon. The claim is narrower and it is about ownership: I cannot construct
a version of this business that supports today's price with evidence that currently
exists.

## 2. Business

### How it creates value

BigBear.ai sells decision-support software and, mostly, **people** to two customer
groups: US national-security agencies (DoD/"Department of War", the intelligence
community, DHS) and "trade and travel" operators (airports, customs authorities,
shipyards). The products are real and specific — BearScan and TrueFace for airport
identity and threat screening, Shipyard AI and ProModel for manufacturing simulation and
digital twins, CargoSeer for cargo inspection, and Ask Sage, a platform giving government
users access to hundreds of commercial AI models inside accredited, classified-capable
environments without data leaving the boundary.

The customer preference being satisfied is **accreditation and clearance, not
capability**. A DoD user cannot put mission data into a commercial LLM. Ask Sage's value
is that it is already through the security paperwork. Most of the employees hold
clearances. That is a genuine service, and it is why customers come here rather than to a
hyperscaler directly.

### How it captures value

Not the way an "AI company" does. From the FY2025 10-K, **61% of revenue is
time-and-material and 25% is fixed-price**. Time-and-material means the company bills
hours — it is a staffing arrangement with a technology wrapper. The economics confirm it:

- **Gross margin 22.3% (FY2025)**, 25.8% TTM. A software business at this revenue runs
  70–80%.
- **Revenue per employee $220k** (579 employees). Software companies run $400–800k+.
- **Cost of revenue was 78% of revenue in FY2025**, up from 71% in FY2024.

There is a real software business inside — Ask Sage — and it is visible in the margin
mix. But it was acquired, not built, and it is 18% of the whole.

### How it protects value

**This is where the answer is weakest, and the silence is the finding.**

Ask Sage's moat is its FedRAMP/IL-level accreditations and its incumbency inside
classified environments. That is a real barrier with a real clock on it: accreditation is
a process every competitor can also complete, and the hyperscalers, Palantir, and the
model vendors themselves are all completing it. It buys years, not decades.

The legacy 82% has **no durable protection, and the filings say so**. Per the 10-K, most
contracts contain **termination-for-convenience provisions**, contracts are **not fully
funded at award**, and the federal government is **prohibited from exercising contract
options more than one year in advance**. A revenue base that the customer may cancel at
will and cannot commit to beyond twelve months is not an annuity.

And the customer table proves it empirically rather than theoretically:

| | FY2023 | FY2024 | FY2025 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Customer A | **$33.27M (21%)** | $21.28M (13%) | **$0 (0%)** |
| Customer B | $24.56M (16%) | $25.68M (16%) | **$10.64M (8%)** |
| Customer C | $18.14M (12%) | $18.73M (12%) | $16.95M (13%) |
| Customer D | — | $0 | $13.83M (11%) |
| Customer E | — | $16.84M (11%) | $20.73M (16%) |
| Customer F | — | $0 | $13.60M (11%) |
| All others | | $75.70M (48%) | $51.93M (41%) |
| **Total** | **$155.16M** | **$158.24M** | **$127.67M** |

The two largest customers of 2023 were **$57.8M combined — 37% of the company**. They are
now **$10.6M**. That is a **$47.2M** loss of revenue against a total decline of $27.5M;
new wins (D and F, each ~11%) refilled part of the hole. This is a **churn-and-replace**
book, not a compounding one. Customer A — a fifth of the company in 2023 — went to
literally zero.

## 3. Threads pulled

### The organic-revenue split management would not say out loud — RESOLVED

On the Q1 2026 call and in the press release, both the CEO and CFO described revenue as
"comparable" year over year, attributing it to "lower volume on Army programs that was
substantially offset by revenue attributable to the Ask Sage acquisition." **Neither
document gives the Ask Sage number.** Since Ask Sage closed 2025-12-31, Q1 2026 is its
first full quarter and Q1 2025 contains none of it — so the split is the whole story and
it was withheld twice.

I first triangulated it from the gross-margin bridge (Q1'25 21.3% → Q1'26 34.0%),
which bounded the Ask Sage contribution at roughly **$6.4M–$9.0M**. Then I found it
disclosed exactly, as ASC 805 requires, in **10-Q Note 4**:

| | |
|---|---|
| Q1 2026 total revenue | $34.40M |
| — Ask Sage contribution | **$6.144M** (17.9%) |
| — legacy business | **$28.26M** |
| Q1 2025 legacy revenue | $34.80M |
| **Organic growth** | **−18.8%** |

The same note gives pro-forma Q1 2025 revenue of **$36.32M** (as if Ask Sage had been
owned), which implies Ask Sage did $1.52M in Q1 2025 and therefore grew **+304%** YoY.
So the like-for-like comparison is **$36.32M → $34.40M = −5.3%**.

Both readings matter and they point the same way: **Ask Sage is genuinely excellent and
genuinely small; the 82% it was bolted onto is falling fast enough to swallow a business
growing 300%.**

My triangulation was directionally right but landed 20–45% too high. The disclosed figure
supersedes it; I record the estimate only to show the estimate was not needed.

**But the YoY figure is backward-looking, and the adversarial pass caught it.** Ask Sage
contributed nothing to any 2025 quarter (it closed 2025-12-31) and CargoSeer is disclosed
as immaterial, so every 2025 quarter *is* legacy and the series is directly comparable:

| | Q1'25 | Q2'25 | Q3'25 | Q4'25 | Q1'26 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Legacy revenue | $34.80M | $32.50M | $33.10M | $27.30M | **$28.26M** |
| Sequential | — | −6.6% | +1.8% | −17.5% | **+3.5%** |

Legacy grew **+3.5% sequentially** into Q1 2026, against a prior-year Q4→Q1 of
**−20.5%** ($43.8M → $34.8M). The −18.8% YoY is real, but it measures against a Q1'25
that still contained the Army revenue that has since gone to zero — it describes a
completed event, not a continuing rate.

The honest reading is that it cuts both ways: Q4'25's $27.3M was itself an anomalous
trough (−17.5% sequential), Q1'26 at $28.26M remains ~14% below the Q2–Q3'25 average of
~$32.8M, and one quarter of sequential improvement off the worst print in years is not a
bottom. But anyone quoting "−18.8% and falling" as a forward rate is over-reading a
backward-looking comp, and that includes the first draft of this document.

### FY2026 guidance vs. the arithmetic

Guidance is **$135–165M** (affirmed at Q1), against FY2025's $127.7M. It **excludes
future acquisitions** by stated company practice. Rolling forward the two disclosed
rates — legacy at −18.8%, Ask Sage at its Q1 run-rate — gives:

```
legacy   $127.7M × 0.812  = $103.7M
Ask Sage $6.144M × 4      =  $24.6M
                            -------
implied FY2026              $128.2M
```

That is **$7M below the bottom of guidance** and $22M below the midpoint. Q1 annualized
is $137.6M, barely clearing the low end. So the guide requires either legacy
stabilization that has not yet appeared in a single quarter, or Ask Sage to keep
compounding hard, or both. The midpoint requires a lot of both. Guidance is not
self-evidently wrong — it is unsupported by the run-rate, which is a different and more
useful statement.

### Backlog quality — the headline number is the wrong number

The press-release headline is "Backlog increased 14% to $281.9 million." The investor
slides give the split the release does not:

| | 4Q25 | 1Q26 |
|---|---|---|
| Total backlog | $248.1M | $281.9M (+14%) |
| **Funded backlog** | $54.9M | **$79.1M (+44%)** |

**Only 28% of backlog is funded.** The rest is contract ceiling and option periods. The
$53M classified award the quarter was built around was described by the CEO himself as a
"**ceiling value**" — an IDIQ ceiling, not committed revenue. The company's own risk
factors name this exact failure: "the failure of contracts comprising backlog to result
in revenue due to changes in funding, terminations for convenience, or **option periods
going unexercised**." Funded backlog of $79.1M against $135–165M of guided revenue means
most of the guided year is not yet funded.

In fairness: funded backlog grew faster (+44%) than total (+14%), which is the healthier
direction. It is the one metric in this filing moving the right way for the right reason.

### The balance-sheet "improvement" was purchased with dilution — not earned

Management's framing is "the strongest financial position in the company's history" and
$431.5M of cash. The slides' own bridge:

|---|---|---|
| Cash & investments | $461.5M | **$431.5M (−$30.0M)** |

Gross cash **fell $30M** in the quarter. Net cash rose $60M only because $124.6M of 2029

### Ask Sage: what $272.1M bought — and it is the strongest thing here

Purchase consideration was **$272.1M**, essentially all cash ($262.4M at closing). For an
asset now running **~$24.6M of annualized revenue**, that is **~11× revenue paid in
cash** — 19% of the company's entire current market cap, funded by the equity raises
above. Goodwill rose $119.1M → $241.1M.

I want to be fair to this: **+304% YoY growth, the entire 1,278bp gross-margin
expansion, and a defensible accreditation position** make Ask Sage a good asset, and
buying it was arguably the best capital-allocation decision in the company's history.
The problem is not Ask Sage. The problem is that it is 18% of a whole whose other 82% is
shrinking at 19%.

### Threads that went nowhere — recorded, because they were checked

  The forward-looking risk list in both the press release and the slides mentions "our
  ability to remediate a material weakness in our internal control over financial
  reporting," which reads like a live finding. It is not. The FY2025 10-K states that the

  businesses acquired in FY2025 (i.e. Ask Sage) are permissibly **excluded from the scope

- **Commitments and contingencies — DEAD END.** Note 16 (10-K) / the 10-Q equivalent
  discloses no purchase obligations, no take-or-pay, no capacity commitments; legal
  accrual is $0.3M. Trailing capex is **$764k**. So the asset-light claim survives the

- **Estimate-vs-actual EPS pattern — LOW INFORMATION, do not use.** The broker series

  matches GAAP exactly (62.0/252.3 = −0.246). Second, EPS on a share count that nearly
  doubled improves arithmetically even when the dollar loss does not shrink — and the

- **Post-call event sweep — nothing material.** Since the 2026-05-05 call and the

  approval for Pangiam threat detection (2026-07-09). Both are product press releases

  management's framing. Next hard datapoint is the Q2 print.
- **Disclosure cadence.** The stockanalysis transcript corpus holds 15 events from

- **Call structure.** The Q1 2026 call had **no Q&A** — six speaker turns, operator to
  operator, and the operator's opening offered star-zero only for *operator assistance*,

  Neither is improper; together they mean **no adversarial party has questioned management
  on the record about any of the above**, which is part of why the organic split stayed
  buried in a footnote.

## 4. Valuation

### The reverse-DCF refuses this input, and that refusal is the finding

```
$ uv run python -m tools.valuation.reverse_dcf --market-cap 1408111383 \
    --base-fcf -54052000 --growth 0.05 0.05 0.05 --terminal-growth 0.02
refused: base_fcf must be positive, got -54052000.0        (exit 2)
```

TTM FCF is **−$54.1M**, and it has been negative in every year of the company's public
life (−20.4, −49.7, −18.3, −38.6, −42.5, TTM −54.1). There is no implied return to solve
for. Anything else would be me choosing a multiple and calling it arithmetic.

### So I inverted the question instead: what would it have to become?

Solving the Gordon relation for the steady-state FCF that supports today's $1.408B
market cap, **assuming no further dilution**:

| Required return | Terminal g | Steady-state FCF needed |
|---|---|---|
| 8% | 2.5% | $77M |
| **10%** | **2.5%** | **$106M** |
| 12% | 2.5% | $134M |
| 15% | 2.5% | $176M |

Taking the 10% case and asking what revenue produces $106M of FCF:

| FCF margin | Revenue required | vs. today's $127.4M |
|---|---|---|
| 25% (excellent software) | $422M | **3.3×** |
| 15% (good software) | $704M | **5.5×** |
| 8% (defense services) | $1,320M | **10.4×** |

So at a 10% required return, today's price implies BigBear.ai roughly **triples revenue
while achieving best-in-class software margins** — from a base that is *shrinking 18.8%
organically*, at a 22–26% gross margin, with 61% of revenue billed time-and-material,
and without issuing any of the 521M shares it just authorized. Every one of those is a
separate thing that has to go right.

**Assumptions, stated plainly:** market cap $1,408,111,383 and TTM FCF −$54.052M from
`/stocks/BBAI/statistics/` (`hover` fields, 2026-07-21); Gordon perpetuity, i.e. a
constant-growth steady state reached at an unspecified future date with the interim path
ignored — which **flatters** the result, since the interim path is cash-burning and
dilutive; no further share issuance, which is the least defensible assumption in the
table given the 2026-06-09 authorization. This is not a reverse-DCF. It is
order-of-magnitude arithmetic, and I am not quoting a decimal from it.

## 5. Load-bearing conditions — **4**

The pass rests on exactly these. If all four fail, I am wrong.

*(Conditions 1 and 4 are restated below from the first draft — the Phase 5 pass showed
both were worded more strongly than the evidence carries. The originals are struck
through so the correction is visible rather than quietly absorbed.)*

1. ~~The legacy 82% keeps declining.~~ → **The legacy 82% has not durably stabilized.**
   Sourced at −18.8% YoY (10-Q Note 4), but **+3.5% sequentially** off a trough. Effective
   **n = 1 quarter in each direction.** Survives the attack; materially weakened by it.
2. **Ask Sage cannot outgrow the decline fast enough.** At 18% of revenue growing 304%
   against 82% shrinking 19%, the crossover is years away *and* requires the growth rate
   to hold as the base enlarges. **← this is the UNKNOWN one (§7, §8).**
3. **Dilution continues.** The 2026-06-09 authorization is capacity, not a commitment —
   an inference from conduct, not a disclosed plan. **Survives, and strengthened** by the
   incentive check: no per-share, FCF, EPS or TSR metric exists in either pay plan, and
   the January 2026 note conversion chose dilution over $431M of available cash.
4. ~~No path from −$54M FCF to ~$106M.~~ → **No *evidenced* path from −$54M FCF to the
   ~$74.5M the operating stub requires.** The $106M figure charged the full market cap
   against the business; net of $414.9M cash the requirement is **$74.5M**, which an
   aggressive Ask Sage path reaches by year 4. A path exists arithmetically; what is
   absent is evidence for it. Survives only in this weakened form.

Note what is *not* load-bearing: the moat question, governance conduct, the missing Q&A,
the disclosure-cadence gap. Those are colour that made me look harder. Delete all of them
and conditions 1–4 still carry the pass.

## 6. Falsifiers — what would make me revisit

I would take another serious look on any of:

- **Two consecutive quarters of positive organic YoY revenue growth**, ex-acquisitions,
  disclosed with the ASC 805 split. This is the single cleanest one.
- **Adjusted EBITDA positive** without the adjustments doing the work — i.e. positive
  *operating* cash flow, not a non-GAAP construct. Q1 2026 adjusted EBITDA was −$9.9M and
  *widening* from −$7.0M.
- **Share count growth under ~5% annualized** for a full year. That would falsify
  condition 3 directly and would mean the 1.0B authorization was housekeeping after all.
- **Funded backlog above ~50% of total** (from 28%), which would make the backlog headline

## 8. Kill-thesis verdict

**Verdict: UNPROVEN.** `conditions=4 refuted=0 unknown=1`

Nothing was refuted. But **condition 2 could not be attacked at all**, because the
evidence needed to resolve it does not exist in any disclosure. That is the definition of
this verdict, not a failure to reach one.

### Per condition

| # | Condition | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Legacy has not durably stabilized | **Survives, materially weakened** |
| 2 | Ask Sage cannot outgrow the decline | **UNKNOWN — unverifiable** |
| 3 | Dilution continues | **Survives, strengthened** |
| 4 | No evidenced path to the required FCF | **Survives, weakened; rewording forced** |

**Condition 2 is the one that stopped the attack.** I attacked it with arithmetic — on an
aggressive-but-decelerating path (Ask Sage +150%/+95%/+67%/+50%, legacy
−19%/−15%/−12%/−10%) it **crosses over legacy in year 2** and reaches ~$360M of total
revenue by year 4, which at a 25% FCF margin is ~$90M — comfortably above the $74.5M the
operating stub requires. So the bull case is arithmetically reachable. Whether Ask Sage
actually compounds that way is the entire question, and I have **exactly two data
points**: $6.144M (Q1'26, disclosed) and ~$1.52M (Q1'25, derived from the pro-forma).
Two points immediately after an acquisition is not a growth curve. BBAI discloses no ARR,
no net revenue retention, no customer count, no contract duration, and no segment split —
the `metrics/` route returns nothing for this ticker and `financials/segments/` is
Pro-gated. I could not resolve it in either direction, and under this skill's rule
uncertainty withholds SOUND rather than earning it.

**The attack that came closest to landing** was the sum-of-parts stub. Net of $414.9M
cash the market pays ~$993M for the operating business, which needs $74.5M of
steady-state FCF, not $106M — a **29% reduction in the hurdle**, and it forced condition 4
to be reworded from "no path" (false — a path exists) to "no evidenced path" (defensible).
It survives only on timing: FCF arriving in year 5 rather than immediately raises the
requirement back to **$120M**, which the aggressive path does not reach, and the path
assumes zero use of the 521M shares just authorized. The counter is that cash funding an
operating loss and refilled by issuance is not a claim the shareholder holds — it is one
the shareholder keeps re-buying.

### Standing checks

- **Base rate:** 2021 SPAC-cohort companies with negative FCF, serial dilution, and
  declining organic revenue overwhelmingly do badly; turnarounds usually fail. BBAI is
  ~71% below its ~$10 SPAC reference price. Supports the pass — but a base rate is a
  prior, not a finding about this company.
- **Steelmanned long case:** a $993M stub containing an accredited, classified-capable
  GenAI platform growing 304% inside the most defensible customer moat in software;
  federal AI budgets inflecting (DHS FY26 appropriation signed, $65.8B FY27 naval
  shipbuilding request); funded backlog +44% QoQ; gross margin +1,278bp; $431M to keep
  buying accredited assets; a rebuilt leadership bench; and a legacy decline that is a
  *dated, identified* event (Customer A → $0) which laps out rather than compounding. It
  is a coherent case and it rests on the same unverifiable parameter.
- **Management incentives:** run, and productive — see §3. Revenue-weighted at 35%, no
  per-share/FCF/EPS/TSR metric anywhere, LTI 100% time-vesting RSUs. Strengthens
  condition 3. Offsetting: a 0% payout was taken on the missed revenue metric with no
  goalpost-moving.
- **Disconfirming search:** genuinely ran against the thesis and produced two findings
  that damaged it — the sequential legacy stabilization and the stub arithmetic. Both are
  written into the document above rather than dropped.
- **Moat — mechanism or checkbox:** mechanism (accreditation and clearance incumbency),
  with an explicit clock on it, and the legacy 82% has none. Handled adequately in §2.

### Statistical checks

- **Effective n on the headline claim is 1.** The −18.8% organic figure is a single
  quarter. The +3.5% sequential rebuttal is *also* a single quarter. Neither side of
  condition 1 has more than one observation.
- **The +304% Ask Sage figure is n = 2**, one of which is a pro-forma derivation, off a
  base of $1.52M.
- **No backtest, hit rate, or screen underlies any claim**, so overlapping-window and
  multiple-comparison corrections do not apply. The one signal-derived input —
  `composite`'s +3 — was correctly refused as an endorsement in §9 (coverage 0.167, two
  mechanical signals).

### Options timing check — **NOT APPLICABLE**, and why

The trigger requires a **dated** claim *and* a listed chain. BBAI has a chain, but **the
thesis makes no dated claim**: it is a pass, and every falsifier in §6 is undated ("two
consecutive quarters", "for a full year"). The 2026-08-10 print is a catalyst for
*information*, not a deadline the thesis bets against. Testing it would measure an event
the thesis never asserted. Skipped deliberately, per step 5.

Separately, and independently disqualifying: the §4 read is marked **UNRELIABLE** (the
put fails the spread gate), it used the **path-2 stopgap** because path 1's depth gate is
unreachable, and options evidence is one-way regardless. It could not have moved this
verdict even had the trigger fired.

### Falsifiers

§6 names five, all observable, specific, and dated to disclosures that will actually
occur. This passes — the author has an argument, not a position.

### What would flip the verdict

**Two more quarters of disclosed Ask Sage revenue** — or any ARR, net-revenue-retention,
or customer-count metric — establishing whether the +304% is durable or an
acquisition-year artifact. That single disclosure moves condition 2 from UNKNOWN to
either refuted (→ FLAWED, and the pass is wrong) or survived (→ SOUND). Q2 2026 reports
**2026-08-10 after close**; one more 10-Q Note 4 gets this to n = 3.
