# Eos Energy Enterprises (EOSE) — 2026-07-22

Spot **$4.165** · market cap **$1.47B** · EV **$1.73B** · next earnings **2026-08-05, before open**

---

## 1. Verdict and thesis

**PASS.** Eos may well succeed at building a real American long-duration battery
business — and at today's price the equity still doesn't pay you for it: one share
bought now needs revenue to compound **~62–70%/yr for five straight years merely to
return zero**, and that is before two load-bearing facts that no disclosure contains.

**kill-thesis verdict: FLAWED — repairable; the PASS conclusion survives.**
Five load-bearing conditions, **2 refuted**, **3 UNKNOWN**. The two refuted arguments
were mine, not the bull's, and both are struck from this write-up (see §3). What
remains is the valuation condition, which survived every attack I could mount.

This is deliberately *not* a "company is failing" thesis. The bear case that reads
well — cash crisis, imminent insolvency — does not survive contact with the filings.
The durable objection is narrower and harder to argue away: **price**.

## 2. Business

### How it creates value
Eos sells the **Z3**, a *static aqueous zinc-halide* battery module (not a flow
battery), packaged into **Indensity** cores and paired with **DawnOS**, its controls
and analytics layer. The customer — a utility, IPP, or increasingly a data-centre
developer — buys grid storage that discharges for **4 to 16 hours**, versus the 2–4
hours where lithium is strongest.

Four things are genuinely being bought, and only the first is about electrons:

1. **Duration.** 55% of Eos's quoted pipeline is 8-hour-plus, a band where lithium's
   economics degrade because you pay for energy capacity you cycle rarely.
2. **Domestic content.** ~90% of the bill of materials is US-sourced (company claim,
   [JPM conf, 2026-06-23]). With the Section 301 tariff on Chinese non-EV lithium
   cells at **25% since 2026-01-01** and FEOC rules unsettled, this is a live
   commercial advantage — and a policy-dependent one.
3. **Safety.** Zinc-halide has no thermal-runaway pathway. Independent fire testing
   (commissioned by Eos, conducted by a third party) reported no propagation.
4. **Siting density.** Indensity stacks vertically — a claimed 250 MWh/acre at
   3-high up to ~1 GWh/acre at 12-high.

### How it captures value
Equipment sale at roughly **$225–250/kWh** — derived, since the company won't state
ASP: backlog $645M ÷ 2.6 GWh ≈ **$248/kWh**; pipeline $24B ÷ 107 GWh ≈ **$224/kWh**.
Plus AC scope (transformers, inverters), commissioning, long-term maintenance plans,
and DawnOS retrofits onto the installed base. Since May 2026 there is a fifth,
materially different channel: **selling to Frontier Power USA, an affiliate it
part-funds** (§3).

### How it protects value — the honest answer
**Weakly, and mostly by policy rather than by mechanism.** Tariffs and FEOC rules are
not a moat; they are a subsidy that can reverse with an election. The 45X credit
already **phases down from 2030 and ends 2033**.

The only durable mechanism would be a genuine cost or performance edge, and that
reduces to round-trip efficiency — which is **UNKNOWN** (§6). Zinc-bromine chemistry
is not proprietary; the barrier is manufacturing execution, which is a lead, not a
moat. **Redflow, the other prominent commercial zinc-bromine manufacturer, entered
administration in August 2024 and was liquidated that December.** That is what an
unprotected position in this chemistry looks like when financing stops.

Where the case rests on electrochemistry I cannot adjudicate it, and I have not
pretended to. See §6.

## 3. Threads pulled

### Frontier Power USA — the thread that mattered
Announced 2026-05-13. Cerberus (already Eos's largest shareholder, its DDTL lender,
and its Series B preferred holder) and Eos jointly capitalise **Frontier Power USA**,
targeting ~$375M of equity levered ~5:1 behind an insurance wrap ≈ $2B of project
capital. FPUSA then places a **2 GWh capacity reservation with Eos**, and Eos books
the resulting revenue as a **related-party line**, while holding ~49% of FPUSA's
returns.

Eos funded its contribution by selling stock: **$75M registered direct to Hudson Bay
Capital at $5.481 (closed 2026-07-01)** plus a **$150M rights offering at $5.481**
(expired 2026-07-21). So Eos raised ~$225M from its own shareholders — roughly **60%
of the equity of its own customer** — in exchange for 49% of the returns, while
gross-margin-negative.

Captive finance is ordinary (GE Capital, Cat Financial). This is not the ordinary
version: the counterparty is the controlling creditor, the funding comes from an
equity raise earmarked for the purpose, the economics split 60/49 against Eos, and
**480 MWh of the "orders" announced in the following weeks were to this affiliate.**
As of the Q1 10-Q it was still only a *binding term sheet*, not a definitive
agreement. This is not fraud and not necessarily improper — it is **lower-quality
revenue**, and it should be discounted as such.

### Dead end — "the rights offering failed" (I was wrong)
I initially concluded the rights offering was deeply underwater: subscription price
$5.481 against a $4.24 close on expiry day, no disclosed backstop, stock down 30%
during the subscription window. **That reasoning was wrong** — it valued the warrant
at zero. Each unit is 1 share **+ 0.4388 of a TEN-YEAR warrant** struck at $5.481.

| 10-year vol | warrant | ×0.4388 | unit value | vs $5.481 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 60% | $2.89 | $1.27 | $5.51 | +0.5% |
| 70% | $3.18 | $1.40 | $5.64 | +2.8% |
| 90% | $3.63 | $1.59 | $5.83 | +6.4% |

**Break-even 10-year implied vol: 58.1%** — below this stock's own RV20 of 71.4%.
The unit was in the money at expiry. Recorded because it is the kind of error that
otherwise propagates: *a discounted rights offering is not underwater until you have
priced every component of the unit.*

### Dead end — "EOSE is priced at a 3× premium to lithium" (also wrong)
Comparing Eos's ~$248/kWh against BNEF's **$70/kWh LFP pack** price (down 45% YoY,
2025) is a category error: a pack is a component, an Eos system is installed capacity.
Against the right comparators the conclusion inverts — global **turnkey** BESS
averaged ~$117/kWh in 2025, and **Tesla Megapack lists ~$266/kWh**, *above* Eos. For a
tariff- and FEOC-constrained US buyer, **Eos is price-competitive.** The lithium
deflation trend is still a genuine headwind (BNEF forecasts a further ~3% decline in
2026) but it is not the knockout I first took it for.

### The accounting trap — a +$826M "profit" caused by the stock falling
`/statistics/` reports TTM net income of **−$1,013,340,000**; `/financials/` reports
**+$826,557,000**. A **$1.84B sign flip**. Reconciling against quarterly columns:
`/financials/`'s "TTM" net-income cell is just the single Q1'26 quarter copied in, and
summing real quarters gives TTM pretax of −$475.9M, matching `/statistics/` exactly.

The cause is `otherNonOperatingIncome` swinging **±$600M/quarter** — non-cash marks on
Cerberus warrants, legacy 2023 warrants, an embedded conversion derivative in the
Nov-2025 converts, and the fair-valued DDTL (Level 3 liabilities: $1,163.1M →
$492.2M in Q1'26). Q1'26's headline "profit" is the accounting echo of the share price
**falling**. The Q1 cash-flow statement adds back **$577.0M** of non-cash items.
**Operating income is the only line on this income statement that means anything.**

### Debt: the aggregator understates it by ~$300M
stockanalysis reports total debt **$642.9M**. The 10-Q reports carrying value
**$619.5M** but **principal $943.6M** — the $600M Nov-2025 converts are carried at
$372M after an embedded derivative was bifurcated out under ASC 815. **At maturity
Eos owes the face.** Any screen keying on `debt` is reading an accounting artifact.

Structure: DDTL (Cerberus) $197.9M @7%, PIK-electable; DOE $95.8M **100% PIK, no cash
interest until 2028**; May-2025 converts $50M @6.75% (conv. $5.10); Nov-2025 converts
$600M @1.75% (conv. $16.29 — far out of the money, so these get repaid, not
converted). A **Springing Maturity** pulls ~$294M of DDTL+DOE debt forward to
**2030-03-14** if the May-2025 converts are still outstanding.

### Liquidity — less dire than it first looks
The naive read (cash $410.7M ÷ $154.8M quarterly burn ≈ 2.6 quarters) is too harsh.
It ignores $61.7M of restricted cash, the $75M Hudson Bay raise, ~$150M of likely
rights proceeds, **$186.6M of undrawn DOE availability**, and the PIK structure.
Decisively, the **FY2025 10-K states there is "no longer substantial doubt"** about
going concern, and the Q1 10-Q carries no such language. **There is no imminent
solvency wall.** Dilution, not insolvency, is the mechanism that hurts holders here.

## 4. Valuation

`reverse_dcf` **refuses this input** — `refused: base_fcf must be positive, got
-385923000.0`, **exit 2**, a domain refusal, verified by running it. So the question
is inverted into a path-to-steady-state: *what must Eos achieve to justify today's
price?*

**Assumptions, all of them:** one share bought at $4.165 and held to 2031 (5 years);
exit EV/FCF **14×**; net debt at exit **$900M** (today's $943.6M principal plus the
remaining DOE draw, less residual cash — it assumes debt is neither repaid nor
added); exit share count **700M or 900M** (against 353.2M outstanding and 544.8M
already diluted, with authorised raised to 800M and a rights offering just
completed). Dilution is carried per-share: one share today is one share at exit, and
a larger exit count shrinks its claim.

**Revenue required in 2031, base case (14×, 12% FCF margin):**

| Target return | 700M shares | 900M shares |
|---|---|---|
| **0%/yr** (money back) | $2.27B — 14.1× TTM, **70% CAGR** | $2.77B — 17.2× TTM, 77% CAGR |
| 10%/yr | $3.33B — 20.7× TTM, 83% CAGR | $4.13B — 25.7× TTM, 91% CAGR |
| 15%/yr | $4.03B — 25.1× TTM, 90% CAGR | $5.02B — 31.3× TTM, 99% CAGR |

**Bull terms** — 25× exit multiple *and* 15% FCF margin, both aggressive for cyclical
hardware — still require **$1.80B of 2031 revenue for a 15% return**: an 11.2×
increase, a **62% CAGR sustained five years running**, while travelling from a −78%
gross margin to a 15% FCF margin.

Stretching the horizon does not rescue it: 8 years at a 40% CAGR → ~3.3%/yr;
10 years at 35% → ~6.2%/yr.

For scale: **Fluence, the category leader, guides FY2026 revenue of $3.2–3.6B.** The
base case asks Eos to approach Fluence's revenue within five years, at a margin
Fluence has never earned.

**Precision warning.** With ATM IV at 154%, quoting these to a decimal would be
false precision. Read them as **"roughly zero return even if it works, on
assumptions a bull would accept"** — the ranking is robust; the digits are not.

## 5. Falsifiers

**What would make me buy** (i.e. kill this PASS):
- **GAAP** — not adjusted — gross margin positive on 2026-08-05 or 2026-11, and held
- Fixed/variable COGS split disclosed such that gross breakeven arrives at achievable
  volume
- An independent national-lab round-trip-efficiency measurement at or above ~85%
- Named customers under multi-year firm contracts, breaking the 93.3%/three
  concentration
- A capital raise completed at or above market without a discount — evidence the
  financing treadmill has stopped

**What would confirm the PASS and, for a holder, argue for exit:**
- "Adjusted gross margin positive" slips past end-2026, or the adjustment does the work
- The 2027-03-31 minimum-EBITDA/revenue covenants are **deferred a third time**
- DOE declines tranches 2–4 (note tranches 3–4 require a *new* facility satisfactory
  to DOE)
- Backlog falls without an explanation reconciling it to cancellations
- Related-party revenue from FPUSA becomes a material share of total revenue
- Another equity raise before gross breakeven

## 6. UNKNOWNs

1. **Rights-offering subscription result.** Expired 5pm ET 2026-07-21, unannounced as
   of this writing. Resolvable within days via 8-K. On the warrant math it likely
   cleared. **Does not kill the thesis** — it changes cash, not the required CAGR.
2. **Fixed/variable split of COGS.** Disclosed nowhere, and genuinely decisive: on
   Q1'26's $101.4M COGS, a $50M-fixed split still loses money at 4× volume while a
   $70M-fixed split yields **+14% gross margin** at the same volume. Same revenue,
   opposite conclusion. Would come from a segment/cost-structure disclosure the
   company does not make. **This is why the thesis is "priced wrong," not "broken."**
3. **Real-world round-trip efficiency.** Company claims ~85–90% (CEO: 84% on a 1-hour
   cycle, "approaching the 90s" at 10–12h). Independent literature puts the
   zinc-bromine *class* at ~70–82%. PNNL's static zinc-bromine figures trace to a
   single developer — almost certainly Eos itself. A short seller alleges 42–55%.
   **No neutral validation of the Z3 exists.** Would come from a national-lab or
   utility field study. RTE compounds into levelised cost over thousands of cycles,
   so this is load-bearing for the entire value proposition. **Its absence does not
   kill the thesis — it is a reason the required margin of safety is larger than the
   price offers.**
4. **Whether 45X credits sit in revenue or COGS, and how much.** Not verified from a
   filing. Material to reading the gross-margin trend.
5. **Series B anti-dilution reset.** The Series B resets if equity is issued below its
   conversion price; I could not derive that price precisely enough to say whether the
   $5.481 issuance triggered it.

I am not marking the electrochemistry itself UNKNOWN-and-fatal. A moat I cannot
assess is an unassessed moat, not an absent one — but it is counted among the
conditions, not assumed away.
